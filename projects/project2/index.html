Michael Yang — mlyang@ucsd.edu

Proposition: When comparing the development of national railway networks, China surpasses the United States.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Raw count were transformed with a simple function to reflect the percent change from the previous year. Plotting this transformed data over time creates two lines that visually supports the proposition that China's rail lines absolutely grow at a higher rate than the USA's. (Score: 2, fully earnest. I believe this data transformation is a good way to measure growth and a good faith way to compare the development of two different rail systems.)
  • The y-axis clearly communicates that values are measured as percentages. The highest value for China (+18%) and lowest value for the United States (-21%) are highlighted to further cement the proposition in the audience's mind. (Score: 1, somewhat earnest. While callouts can persuade viewers to jump to conclusions without necessarily accounting for the whole picture, these annotations emphasize aspects of the data that are true and support the proposition.)
  • In the title and subtitle for this visuaization, I use carefully-chosen wording to frame the story with a slant favoring the proposition that favors China. (Score: -1, somewhat deceptive. While not untrue, my wording is intentionally one-sided to favor a China's side of this proposition.)

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Plotting the raw count data over time creates two lines that visually supports the proposition that the USA's rail lines continue to dominate as the highest number in the world, with China remaining in 2nd place. (Score: 0, mixed. I believe the raw counts tell a true story of an old rail system remaining the largest in the world, but misleads the viewer by minimizing the significance of China's rail line growth.)
  • Both lines are plotted on a common y-axis, allowing the viewer to compare values across both lines. This creates trust between the viewer and the visualization and contributes to its credibility. (Score: 2, fully earnest. Plotting two lines on a common axis is common practice and good for credibility. Using a dual-axis plot with a different y-axis for each line instead would have been misleading.)
  • In the title and subtitle for this visuaization, I use carefully-chosen wording to frame the story with a slant favoring the proposition that favors the United States and undermines China. (Score: -1, somewhat deceptive. While not untrue, my wording is intentionally one-sided to favor the United States and argue against the proposition.)

Final Reflection

In creating these two visualizations, I was able to explore how the same dataset can produce visualizations on opposite ends of a particular argument or proposition. Creating the graphs themselves was straightforward, but I spent more time than I expected on thinking about what proposition I wanted to tackle, and how I could write arguments for either side of the proposition, and how I can visualize the same dataset to support both of those arguments. I think these two visualizations I've created are both valid, although one is less accurate and more disingenous than the other. I now define “ethical analysis and visualization” as a data processing and data visualization design process that throughly considers the implications of design decisions in persuading an audience to believe one side or another of a proposition. Visual encodings are powerful because viewers will often make up their mind about an issue based on a quick glance at a visual and their initial visual takeways. That's why it's important to really consider how much a visualization persuades a reader and how much is an transparent and accurate reflection of the data. While the lines between “acceptable” persuasive choices and “misleading” ones can be blurry, a good faith interpretation of the data with a designer educated about the context of the data and the intended audience should be able to use any design choices to tell a story that is transparent about shortcomings and makes it as easy as possible for the audience to understand that this is just one interpretation of the data that may contain potential biases. Dependng on which measure is used, it can appear that either China or the United States has a better developed railline system. I want these visualizations to open up a discussion about data metrics: what kinds of data do we collect to measure development or progress and how do we make that data comparable?
